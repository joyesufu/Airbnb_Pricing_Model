About the Project

Using Machine Learning to Predict Rental Pricing

We were challenged to create a machine learning model that would be able to create a prediction based on a given set of training data. We decided on AirBnb's expansive dataset to shape our model and drive our predictions.

Data Analysis with Visualizations


In order to stabilize and enhance the predictive power of our model, we needed to identify any listings in the dataset that were sufficiently unrepresentative of the typical Airbnb listing in New York City. Incorporating a luxury

1-bedroom rental listed at $10,000/night in the outskirts of Staten Island, for example, could obscure some intuitive relationships between the location and size of a rental and its price. As such, we defined various criteria that could be used to exclude unusual listings.

First and foremost, we removed any listings that had less than 20 reviews. This was done for several reasons, but most importantly 1) to weed out new Airbnb rentals on the market that have not had the chance to recalibrate their price to accurately reflect demand, and 2) to enhance the reliability of the ratings data.

We also removed all listings with greater than 10 bedrooms. Listings with a minimum stay requirement of more than one year were similarly excluded.

We also considered the property type of the listing. Inside Airbnb distinguishes between property type and room type, with property type giving a more descriptive label to the listing. For example, a listing may have a room type of ‘hotel room’ but the property type may describe the hotel room as belonging to a ‘boutique hotel’.

We summarized all the listings in New York City and removed from the analysis any listings that had a property type that showed up 5 or fewer times in the dataset. This removed unusual listings with property types of ‘boat’, ‘cave’, and ‘room in a religious building’ for example.

Finally, we wanted to account for listings with atypically high prices which may not be well-explained by the existing data. However, we recognized that what may be an atypically high price in one part of New York City may not be unusually expensive in another part of the city.

As such, we chose to define “atypically expensive” as any rental priced at a daily dollar value of 50% of the maximum listing price in that rental’s borough. In other words, if the most expensive Airbnb rental in Manhattan is $10,000 per night, then any rental greater than $5,000 per night would be designated as atypically expensive.

At the same time, if the most expensive Airbnb in Queens is $4,000 per night, then any listing greater than $2,000 per night would be designated as atypically expensive and removed from the model.

Features Considered

  • Borough
  • Number of bedrooms
  • Number of individuals accommodate
  • Room type (entire home, private bedroom, hotel room)
  • Rental is instantly bookable
  • Rental is a short-term rental (minimum stay of 30 days)
  • Cleanliness Rating
  • Location Rating
  • Value Rating
  • Overall rating

Model

We employed two models to measure the relationship between a listing’s price and the aforementioned features. The first was a standard linear regression model, which attempts to fit a straight line through the data that minimizes the differences between each data point and the line. One of the primary shortcomings of a linear regression model is that it is unable to capture non-linear relationships in the data. We also utilized a random forests regressor model.
